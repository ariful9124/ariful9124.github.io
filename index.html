About Me

I am a PhD researcher in the Department of Systems and Information Engineering at the University of Virginia, where I develop machine learning and signal processing methods to extract clinically meaningful insights from passively collected mobile and wearable sensor data.

My research sits at the intersection of ubiquitous computing, biomedical signal processing, and machine learning, with a focus on building scalable digital health systems that can improve quality of life for individuals living with mental illness, chronic disease, and neuromuscular disorders—without requiring active user effort.

Current Research Focus

Mobile Sensing for Mental Health

Building passive smartphone sensing pipelines to detect early warning signs of depression, anxiety, and psychosis through behavioral patterns.

Wearable Sensing for Neuromuscular Disease

Using IMU-based wearable sensors to quantify movement impairment and track disease progression in ALS and other neuromuscular conditions.

mHealth for Cancer Survivors

Developing smartphone-based tools to monitor quality of life, fatigue, and pain in post-treatment cancer survivors using multimodal passive sensing.
