Research

My research focuses on developing intelligent systems that passively monitor human health and behavior using everyday mobile devices and wearable sensors — making personalized healthcare scalable and accessible.

Research Vision

The proliferation of smartphones and wearables has created an unprecedented opportunity to continuously measure the physiological and behavioral signals that underpin health outcomes. My work builds the machine learning and signal processing infrastructure needed to transform this raw sensing data into actionable clinical insights — enabling early intervention for mental illness, chronic disease, and neurological conditions.

Research Themes

Mobile Sensing for Mental Health

Smartphones collect a rich stream of behavioral signals — GPS traces, communication patterns, screen usage, accelerometer data — that correlate with psychological states. I develop passive sensing pipelines that extract behavioral features from these streams and use supervised and semi-supervised learning to predict depression severity, anxiety episodes, and mood states without any active user effort.

→Key methods: Deep learning on time-series, digital phenotyping, ecological momentary assessment
→Target conditions: Depression, anxiety, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia
→Impact: Enables continuous monitoring between clinical visits for high-risk populations

AI-Driven Health Monitoring

I design multitask and transfer learning frameworks that can jointly predict multiple health outcomes from heterogeneous sensor streams. A key challenge is intervention availability — determining when and how a mobile health (mHealth) system should deliver interventions to maximize user engagement and health impact.

→Key methods: Multitask learning, contextual bandits, federated learning, domain adaptation
→Applications: Just-in-time adaptive interventions (JITAIs), cancer survivorship, chronic pain
→Impact: Improves intervention effectiveness by 20-30% over single-task baselines

Wearable Sensing for Neuromuscular Disease

IMU sensors in smartwatches and body-worn devices capture fine-grained movement data that reflects motor function. I develop signal processing and deep learning methods to extract clinically meaningful gait and motion features for tracking ALS progression, quantifying tremor in Parkinson's disease, and assessing functional decline in neuromuscular disorders.

→Key methods: IMU signal processing, gait analysis, CNN-LSTM architectures
→Target conditions: ALS, Parkinson's disease, multiple sclerosis
→Impact: Enables objective, home-based monitoring to reduce clinical visit burden

Multimodal ML for Behavioral Modeling

Human health is inherently multimodal — combining accelerometry, GPS, audio features, physiology, and text provides a more complete picture than any single modality. I develop late-fusion, cross-modal attention, and foundation model fine-tuning approaches that robustly handle missing modalities in real-world deployments.

→Key methods: Multimodal fusion, self-supervised pretraining, cross-modal transformers, LLM fine-tuning
→Modalities: Motion, GPS, audio, EHR, wearable physiology, free-text EMA
→Challenge: Missing data and modality-specific noise in naturalistic settings

Methodologies

Signal Processing

Filtering, feature extraction, segmentation, spectral analysis of IMU and physiological signals

Deep Learning

Transformers, LSTM, CNN-based architectures for time-series classification and regression

Federated Learning

Privacy-preserving training on distributed health data without centralizing sensitive records

Transfer & Multitask Learning

Leveraging shared representations across tasks and populations to overcome small labeled dataset sizes

Self-Supervised Pretraining

Learning generalizable representations from large unlabeled wearable datasets via contrastive objectives

Study Design & Deployment

IRB protocol development, Android/iOS data collection apps, longitudinal clinical study management
